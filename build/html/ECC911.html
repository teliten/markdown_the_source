In <a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epistle_to_the_Romans” title=”Epistle to the Romans”>Romans</a> 5:12&ndash;21, <a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_of_Tarsus” title=”Paul of Tarsus”>Paul</a> argues that &ldquo;just as through the <a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fall_of_man” title=”Fall of man”>disobedience of the one man</a> the many were made sinners, so also through the <a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Active_obedience_of_Christ” title=”Active obedience of Christ”>obedience of the one man</a> the many will be made righteous&rdquo; (Romans 5:19, <a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NIV” title=”NIV”>NIV</a>). In <a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1_Corinthians_15” title=”1 Corinthians 15”>1 Corinthians 15</a>:22, Paul argues that &ldquo;as in Adam all die, so in Christ all will be made alive,&rdquo; while in verse 45 he calls Jesus the &ldquo;last/ultimate/final Adam&rdquo;.</p>

<p>In <a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Book_of_Exodus” title=”Book of Exodus”>Exodus</a> 31:1-6 and chapters 36 to 39, <strong>Bezalel</strong>, <strong>Bezaleel</strong>, or <strong>Betzalel</strong> (<a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hebrew_language” title=”Hebrew language”>Hebrew</a>: בְּצַלְאֵל, <em>Bəṣalʼēl</em>), was the chief <a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artisan” title=”Artisan”>artisan</a> of the <a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tabernacle” title=”Tabernacle”>Tabernacle</a><a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bezalel#cite_note-1”>[1]</a> and was in charge of building the <a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ark_of_the_Covenant” title=”Ark of the Covenant”>Ark of the Covenant</a>, assisted by <a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aholiab” title=”Aholiab”>Aholiab</a>. The section in chapter 31 describes his selection as chief artisan, in the context of Moses&rsquo; vision of how God wanted the tabernacle to be constructed, and chapters 36 to 39 recount the construction process undertaken by Bezalel, Aholiab and every gifted artisan and willing worker, in accordance with the vision.</p>

<p>Elsewhere in the Bible the name occurs only in the genealogical lists of the <a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Book_of_Chronicles” title=”Book of Chronicles”>Book of Chronicles</a>, but according to <a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cuneiform_script” title=”Cuneiform script”>cuneiform</a> inscriptions a variant form of the same, &ldquo;Ẓil-B&ecirc;l,&rdquo; was borne by a king of <a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaza_City” title=”Gaza City”>Gaza</a> who was a contemporary of <a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hezekiah” title=”Hezekiah”>Hezekiah</a> and <a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manasseh_of_Judah” title=”Manasseh of Judah”>Manasseh</a>.</p>

<p>The name &ldquo;Bezalel&rdquo; means &ldquo;in the shadow [protection] of God.&rdquo; Bezalel is described in the genealogical lists as the son of Uri (Exodus 31:1), the son of <a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hur_(Bible)” title=”Hur (Bible)”>Hur</a>, of the <a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tribe_of_Judah” title=”Tribe of Judah”>tribe of Judah</a> (I <a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Books_of_Chronicles” title=”Books of Chronicles”>Chronicles</a> 2:18, 19, 20, 50). He was said to be highly gifted as a workman, showing great skill and originality in engraving precious metals and stones and in wood-carving.</p>

<p>The phrase <em>puer aeternus</em> comes from <em><a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metamorphoses” title=”Metamorphoses”>Metamorphoses</a></em>, an epic work by the Roman poet <a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ovid” title=”Ovid”>Ovid</a> (43 BC &ndash; c. 17 AD) dealing with Greek and Roman myths. In the poem, Ovid addresses the child-god <a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iacchus” title=”Iacchus”>Iacchus</a> as <em>puer aeternus</em> and praises him for his role in the <a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eleusinian_mysteries” title=”Eleusinian mysteries”>Eleusinian mysteries</a>. Iacchus is later identified with the gods <a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dionysus” title=”Dionysus”>Dionysus</a> and <a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eros” title=”Eros”>Eros</a>. The <em>puer</em> is a god of vegetation and resurrection, the god of divine youth, such as <a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tammuz_(deity)” title=”Tammuz (deity)”>Tammuz</a>, <a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Attis” title=”Attis”>Attis</a>, and <a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adonis” title=”Adonis”>Adonis</a>.<a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Puer_aeternus#cite_note-2”>[2]</a> The figure of a young god who is slain and resurrected also appears in Egyptian mythology as the story of <a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osiris” title=”Osiris”>Osiris</a>.</p>

<p>Swiss <a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Psychiatrist” title=”Psychiatrist”>psychiatrist</a> <a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Jung” title=”Carl Jung”>Carl Gustav Jung</a> developed a school of thought called <a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytical_psychology” title=”Analytical psychology”>analytical psychology</a>, distinguishing it from the <a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Psychoanalysis” title=”Psychoanalysis”>psychoanalysis</a> of <a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sigmund_Freud” title=”Sigmund Freud”>Sigmund Freud</a> (1856&ndash;1939). In analytical psychology (often called &ldquo;Jungian psychology&rdquo;) the <em>puer aeternus</em> is an example of what Jung called an <a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archetype” title=”Archetype”>archetype</a>, one of the &ldquo;primordial, structural elements of the human psyche&rdquo;.<a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Puer_aeternus#cite_note-3”>[3]</a></p>

<p>The <a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shadow_(psychology)” title=”Shadow (psychology)”>shadow</a> of the <em>puer</em> is the <em><a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Senex” title=”Senex”>senex</a></em> (Latin for &ldquo;old man&rdquo;), associated with the god <a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cronus” title=”Cronus”>Cronus</a>&mdash;disciplined, controlled, responsible, rational, ordered. Conversely, the shadow of the <em>senex</em> is the <em>puer</em>, related to <a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermes” title=”Hermes”>Hermes</a> or <a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dionysus” title=”Dionysus”>Dionysus</a>&mdash;unbounded instinct, disorder, intoxication, whimsy.<a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Puer_aeternus#cite_note-Sharp,_p.110-4”>[4]</a></p>

<p>Like all archetypes, the <em>puer</em> is bi-polar, exhibiting both a &ldquo;positive&rdquo; and a &ldquo;negative&rdquo; aspect. The &ldquo;positive&rdquo; side of the <em>puer</em> appears as the Divine Child who symbolizes newness, potential for growth, hope for the future. He also foreshadows the hero that he sometimes becomes (e.g. <a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heracles” title=”Heracles”>Heracles</a>). The &ldquo;negative&rdquo; side is the child-man who refuses to grow up and meet the challenges of life face on, waiting instead for his ship to come in and solve all his problems.</p>

<blockquote> <p>&ldquo;For the time being one is doing this or that&hellip; it is <em>not yet</em> what is really wanted, and there is always the fantasy that sometime in the future the real thing will come about&hellip; The one thing dreaded throughout by such a type of man is to be bound to anything whatever.&rdquo;<a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Puer_aeternus#cite_note-5”>[5]</a></p> </blockquote>

<p>C.G. Jung wrote a paper on the <em>puer aeternus</em>, &ldquo;The Psychology of the Child Archetype&rdquo;, contained in Part IV of <em>The Archetypes and the Collective Unconscious</em> (Collected Works, Vol. 9i). The hero-child aspect and his relationship to the <a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mother_goddess” title=”Mother goddess”>Great Mother</a> is dealt with in chapters 4 and 5 of Part Two of <em>Symbols of Transformation</em> (Collected Works, Vol. 5).<a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Puer_aeternus#cite_note-7”>[7]</a> In his essay &ldquo;Answer to Job&rdquo; (contained in <em>Psychology and Religion: West and East</em>, Vol. 11 of the Collected Works; but also published separately) Jung refers to the <em>puer aeternus</em> as a figure representing the future psychological development of human beings.</p>

<p>&ldquo;</p>

<p>That higher and &lsquo;complete&rsquo; (<em>teleios</em>) man is begotten by the &lsquo;unknown&rsquo; father and born from Wisdom, and it is he who, in the figure of the <em>puer aeternus</em>&mdash;&lsquo;vultu mutabilis albus et ater&rsquo;<a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Puer_aeternus#cite_note-8”>[8]</a>&mdash;represents our totality, which transcends consciousness. It was this boy into whom <a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Faust” title=”Faust”>Faust</a> had to change, abandoning his inflated onesidedness which saw the devil only outside. Christ&rsquo;s &lsquo;Except ye become as little children&rsquo; prefigures this change, for in them the opposites lie close together; but what is meant is the boy who is born from the maturity of the adult man, and not the unconscious child we would like to remain.&quot;<a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Puer_aeternus#cite_note-9”>[9]</a><br /> <em>Now or Neverland</em> is a 1998 book written by Jungian analyst Ann Yeoman dealing with the <em>puer aeternus</em> in the form of <a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_Pan” title=”Peter Pan”>Peter Pan</a>, one of the most well-known examples of the concept in the modern era. The book is a psychological overview of the eternal boy archetype, from its ancient roots to contemporary experience, including a detailed interpretation of <a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J._M._Barrie” title=”J. M. Barrie”>J. M. Barrie</a>&rsquo;s popular play and novel.</p>

<p>Mythologically, Peter Pan is linked to [&hellip;] the young god who dies and is reborn&hellip;as well as to Mercury/Hermes, <a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Psychopomp” title=”Psychopomp”>psychopomp</a> and messenger of the gods who moves freely between the divine and human realms, and, of course, to the great goat-god <a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pan_(mythology)” title=”Pan (mythology)”>Pan</a> [&hellip;] In early performances of Barrie&rsquo;s play, Peter Pan appeared on stage with both pipes and a live goat. Such undisguised references to the <a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chthonic” title=”Chthonic”>chthonic</a>, often lascivious and far from childlike goat-god were, not surprisingly, soon excised from both play and novel.&quot;<a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Puer_aeternus#cite_note-11”>[11]</a><br /> <strong>Dionysus</strong><a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dionysus#cite_note-2”>[a]</a> is the god of the grape-harvest, winemaking and wine, of fertility, ritual madness, <a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Religious_ecstasy” title=”Religious ecstasy”>religious ecstasy</a>, and theatre in <a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Religion_in_ancient_Greece” title=”Religion in ancient Greece”>ancient Greek religion</a> and <a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_mythology” title=”Greek mythology”>myth</a>.<a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dionysus#cite_note-3”>[2]</a><a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dionysus#cite_note-4”>[3]</a></p>

<p>He is also known as <strong>Bacchus</strong> (<a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/English” title=”Help:IPA/English”>/ˈb&aelig;kəs/</a> or <a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/English” title=”Help:IPA/English”>/ˈbɑːkəs/</a>; <a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_language” title=”Greek language”>Greek</a>: &Beta;ά&kappa;&chi;&omicron;&sigmaf;, <em>B&aacute;kkhos</em>), the name adopted by the <a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Rome” title=”Ancient Rome”>Romans</a><a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dionysus#cite_note-5”>[4]</a> and the frenzy he induces is <em>bakkheia</em>. His <em><a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thyrsus” title=”Thyrsus”>thyrsus</a></em>, sometimes wound with ivy and dripping with honey, is both a beneficent wand and a weapon used to destroy those who oppose his cult and the freedoms he represents. As <strong>Eleutherios</strong> (&ldquo;the liberator&rdquo;), his wine, music and ecstatic dance free his followers from self-conscious fear and care, and subvert the oppressive restraints of the powerful. Those who partake of his mysteries are believed to become possessed and empowered by the god himself.<a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dionysus#cite_note-6”>[5]</a></p>

<p>In his religion, identical with or closely related to <a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orphism_(religion)” title=”Orphism (religion)”>Orphism</a>, Dionysus was believed to have been born from the union of <a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zeus” title=”Zeus”>Zeus</a> and <a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Persephone” title=”Persephone”>Persephone</a>, and to have himself represented a chthonic or underworld aspect of Zeus. Many believed that he had been born twice, having been killed and reborn as the son of Zeus and the mortal <a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semele” title=”Semele”>Semele</a>. In the <a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eleusinian_Mysteries” title=”Eleusinian Mysteries”>Eleusinian Mysteries</a> he was identified with <a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iacchus” title=”Iacchus”>Iacchus</a>, the son (or, alternately, husband) of <a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Demeter” title=”Demeter”>Demeter</a>.</p>

<p>His origins are uncertain, and <a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cult_of_Dionysus” title=”Cult of Dionysus”>his cults</a> took many forms; some are described by ancient sources as <a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thrace” title=”Thrace”>Thracian</a>, others as Greek.<a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dionysus#cite_note-7”>[6]</a><a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dionysus#cite_note-8”>[7]</a><a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dionysus#cite_note-9”>[8]</a> Though most accounts say he was born in Thrace, traveled abroad, and arrived in Greece as a foreigner, evidence from the Mycenaean period of Greek history show that he is one of Greece&rsquo;s oldest attested gods. His attribute of &ldquo;foreignness&rdquo; as an arriving outsider-god may be inherent and essential to his cults, as he is a god of <a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theophany” title=”Theophany”>epiphany</a>, sometimes called &ldquo;the god that comes&rdquo;.<a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dionysus#cite_note-10”>[9]</a></p>

<p><a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wine” title=”Wine”>Wine</a> played an important role in Greek culture, and the cult of Dionysus was the main religious focus surrounding its consumption.<a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dionysus#cite_note-11”>[10]</a> Wine, as well as the vines and grapes that produce it, were seen as not only a gift of the god, but a symbolic incarnation of him on earth.<a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dionysus#cite_note-julian-12”>[11]</a> However, rather than being a god of drunkenness, as he was often stereotyped in the post-Classical era, the religion of Dionysus centered on the correct consumption of wine, which could ease suffering and bring joy, as well as inspire divine madness distinct from drunkenness.<a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dionysus#cite_note-iconography-13”>[12]</a> Performance art and drama were also central to his religion, and its festivals were the initial driving force behind the <a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_theatre” title=”History of theatre”>development of theatre</a>.<a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dionysus#cite_note-Brockett-14”>[13]</a> The cult of Dionysus is also a &ldquo;cult of the souls&rdquo;; his maenads feed the dead through blood-offerings, and he acts as a divine communicant between the living and the dead.<a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dionysus#cite_note-15”>[14]</a> He is sometimes categorised as a <a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dying-and-rising_god” title=”Dying-and-rising god”>dying-and-rising god</a>.<a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dionysus#cite_note-corrente2012-16”>[15]</a></p>

<p>Both Hades and Dionysus were associated with a divine tripartite deity with <a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zeus” title=”Zeus”>Zeus</a>.<a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dionysus#cite_note-:2-135”>[134]</a><a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dionysus#cite_note-Rigoglioso-136”>[135]</a><a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dionysus#cite_note-137”>[136]</a> Zeus, like Dionysus, was occasionally believed to have an underworld form, closely identified with Hades, to the point that they were occasionally thought of as the same god.<a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dionysus#cite_note-Rigoglioso-136”>[135]</a></p>

<p>According to Marguerite Rigoglioso, Hades is Dionysus, and this dual god was believed by the Eleusinian tradition to have impregnated Persephone. This would bring the Eleusinian in harmony with the myth in which Zeus, not Hades, impregnated Persephone to bear the first Dionysus. Rigoglioso argues that taken together, these myths suggest a belief that is that, with Persephone, Zeus/Hades/Dionysus created (in terms quoted from Ker&eacute;nyi) &ldquo;a second, a little Dionysus,&rdquo; who is also a &ldquo;subterranean Zeus.&rdquo;<a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dionysus#cite_note-Rigoglioso-136”>[135]</a> The unification of Hades, Zeus, and Dionysus as a single tripartite god was used to represent the birth, death and resurrection of a deity and to unify the &lsquo;shining&rsquo; realm of Zeus and the dark underworld realm of Hades.<a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dionysus#cite_note-:2-135”>[134]</a> According to Rosemarie Taylor-Perry, &ldquo;it is often mentioned that Zeus, Hades and Dionysus were all attributed to being the exact same god&hellip; Being a tripartite deity Hades is also Zeus, doubling as being the Sky God or Zeus, Hades abducts his &lsquo;daughter&rsquo; and paramour Persephone. The taking of Kore by Hades is the act which allows the conception and birth of a second integrating force: Iacchos (Zagreus-Dionysus), also known as Liknites, the helpless infant form of that Deity who is the unifier of the dark underworld (chthonic) realm of Hades and the Olympian (&ldquo;Shining&rdquo;) one of Zeus.&rdquo;<a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dionysus#cite_note-:2-135”>[134]</a><a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dionysus#cite_note-Rigoglioso-136”>[135]</a></p>

<p><b>Menoetius</b>&nbsp;or&nbsp;<b>Menoetes</b>&nbsp;(<a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/English” title=”Help:IPA/English”>/məˈniːʃiəs/</a>;&nbsp;<a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_language” title=”Greek language”>Greek</a>:&nbsp;&Mu;&epsilon;&nu;&omicron;ί&tau;&iota;&omicron;&sigmaf;, &Mu;&epsilon;&nu;&omicron;ί&tau;&eta;&sigmaf;&nbsp;<i>Menoitios</i>), meaning&nbsp;<i>doomed might</i>, is a name that refers to three distinct beings from&nbsp;<a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_mythology” title=”Greek mythology”>Greek mythology</a>:</p>

<ul>

<li>Menoetius, a second generation Titan, son of&nbsp;<a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iapetus_(mythology)” title=”Iapetus (mythology)”>Iapetus</a>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clymene_(mythology)” title=”Clymene (mythology)”>Clymene</a>&nbsp;or&nbsp;<a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asia_(mythology)” title=”Asia (mythology)”>Asia</a>, and a brother of&nbsp;<a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atlas_(mythology)” title=”Atlas (mythology)”>Atlas</a>,&nbsp;<a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prometheus” title=”Prometheus”>Prometheus</a>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epimetheus_(mythology)” title=”Epimetheus (mythology)”>Epimetheus</a>. Menoetius was killed by&nbsp;<a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zeus” title=”Zeus”>Zeus</a>&nbsp;on&nbsp;<a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mount_Triphyle” title=”Mount Triphyle”>Mount Triphyle</a>&nbsp;with a flash of lightning in the&nbsp;<a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Titanomachy” title=”Titanomachy”>Titanomachy</a>, and banished to&nbsp;<a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tartarus” title=”Tartarus”>Tartarus</a>.<sup id=”cite_ref-1”><a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Menoetius#cite_note-1”>[1]</a></sup><sup id=”cite_ref-2”><a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Menoetius#cite_note-2”>[2]</a></sup><sup id=”cite_ref-3”><a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Menoetius#cite_note-3”>[3]</a></sup>&nbsp;His name means &quot;doomed might,&quot; deriving from the Ancient Greek words&nbsp;<i>menos</i>&nbsp;(&quot;might, power&quot;) and&nbsp;<i>oitos</i>&nbsp;(&quot;doom, pain&quot;). Hesiod described Menoetius as&nbsp;<a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubris” title=”Hubris”>hubristic</a>, meaning exceedingly prideful and impetuous to the very end. From what his name suggests, along with Hesiod's own account, Menoetius was perhaps the Titan god of violent anger and rash action.<sup id=”cite_ref-Smiley_4-0”><a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Menoetius#cite_note-Smiley-4”>[4]</a></sup></li> <li>Menoetius, guard of the cattle of Hades. During&nbsp;<a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heracles” title=”Heracles”>Heracles</a>&nbsp;twelfth labor, which required him to steal the hound&nbsp;<a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cerberus” title=”Cerberus”>Cerberus</a>&nbsp;from the Underworld, he slays one of Hades' cattle. A certain Menoetius, son of&nbsp;<a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Keuthonymos” title=”Keuthonymos”>Keuthonymos</a>, challenges Heracles to a wrestling match, during which Heracles hugs him and breaks his ribs before&nbsp;<a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Persephone” title=”Persephone”>Persephone</a>&nbsp;intervenes.<sup id=”cite_ref-5”><a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Menoetius#cite_note-5”>[5]</a></sup></li> <li>Menoetius from&nbsp;<a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Opuntian_Locris” title=”Opuntian Locris”>Opus</a>&nbsp;was one of the&nbsp;<a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Argonauts” title=”Argonauts”>Argonauts</a>, and son of&nbsp;<a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Actor_(mythology)” title=”Actor (mythology)”>Actor</a><sup id=”cite_ref-6”><a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Menoetius#cite_note-6”>[6]</a></sup>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aegina_(mythology)” title=”Aegina (mythology)”>Aegina</a>. He was the father of&nbsp;<a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patroclus” title=”Patroclus”>Patroclus</a>&nbsp;and&nbsp;<a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Myrto_(mythology)” title=”Myrto (mythology)”>Myrto</a>&nbsp;by either&nbsp;<a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Damocrateia&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1” title=”Damocrateia (page does not exist)”>Damocrateia</a><sup id=”cite_ref-7”><a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Menoetius#cite_note-7”>[7]</a></sup>,&nbsp;<a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sthenele” title=”Sthenele”>Sthenele</a>,&nbsp;<a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Periopis&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1” title=”Periopis (page does not exist)”>Periopis</a>&nbsp;or&nbsp;<a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polymele” title=”Polymele”>Polymele</a>.<sup id=”cite_ref-8”><a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Menoetius#cite_note-8”>[8]</a></sup><sup id=”cite_ref-9”><a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Menoetius#cite_note-9”>[9]</a></sup>&nbsp;Among the settlers of Locris, Menoetius was chiefly honored by King&nbsp;<a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Opus_(mythology)” title=”Opus (mythology)”>Opus II</a>, son of Zeus and&nbsp;<a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protogeneia” title=”Protogeneia”>Protogeneia</a>.<sup id=”cite_ref-10”><a href=”http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Menoetius#cite_note-10”>[10]</a></sup></li>
