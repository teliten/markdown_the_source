<p><span style=”font-size:10px;”>In the early days of the rebellion, Judah received a surname Maccabee. Several explanations have been put forward for this surname. Others believe it is in reference to his weapon of choice.</span></p>

<p><span style=”font-size:10px;”>It is also possible that the name Maccabee is an <a href=”https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acronym” title=”Acronym”>acronym</a> for the <a href=”https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torah” title=”Torah”>Torah</a> verse <em>Mi kamokha ba&rsquo;elim Adonai</em>, &ldquo;Who among the gods is like you, O Adonai?&rdquo;, his battle-cry to motivate troops. (<a href=”https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Book_of_Exodus” title=”Book of Exodus”>Exodus</a> 15:11). Rabbi Moshe Schreiber writes that it is an acronym for his father&rsquo;s name Mattityahu Kohen Ben Yochanan. Some scholars maintain that the name is a shortened form of the Hebrew <em>maqqab-ya &macr;h&ucirc;</em> (from <em>na &macr;qab</em>, &lsquo;&lsquo;to mark, to designate&rsquo;&rsquo;), meaning &lsquo;&lsquo;the one designated by Yahweh.&rsquo;<a href=”https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Judas_Maccabeus#cite_note-3”>[3]</a></span></p>

<p>This is a list of <strong>last <a href=”https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/scion” title=”wikt:scion”>scions</a></strong> or <strong>individuals who were the last member of a ruling house</strong>, or other prominent family, where heredity is the prime form of inheritance. This may be the last person to rule a realm, sometimes leading to a political crisis, or a change in government; other times power has already passed from the patrilineal family, leaving it in a less important position when it reaches its extinction.</p>

<p>The <strong>Hasmonean dynasty</strong><a href=”https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hasmonean_dynasty#cite_note-7”>[4]</a> (<a href=”https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/English” title=”Help:IPA/English”>/ˌh&aelig;zməˈniːən/</a> (<a href=”http://www.m-w.com/cgi-bin/audio.pl?hasmon01.wav=Hasmonean”>audio</a>); <a href=”https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hebrew_language” title=”Hebrew language”>Hebrew</a>: חַשְׁמוֹנַּאִים, <em>Ḥa&scaron;mona&rsquo;īm</em>) was a ruling <a href=”https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dynasty” title=”Dynasty”>dynasty</a> of <a href=”https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Judea” title=”Judea”>Judea</a> and surrounding regions during <a href=”https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_antiquity” title=”Classical antiquity”>classical antiquity</a>. Between c.&thinsp;140 and c.&thinsp;116 BCE the dynasty ruled Judea semi-autonomously from the <a href=”https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_Seleucid_rulers” title=”List of Seleucid rulers”>Seleucids</a>. From 110 BCE, with the <a href=”https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seleucid_Empire” title=”Seleucid Empire”>Seleucid Empire</a> disintegrating, the dynasty became fully independent, expanded into the neighbouring regions of <a href=”https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Samaria” title=”Samaria”>Samaria</a>, <a href=”https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galilee” title=”Galilee”>Galilee</a>, <a href=”https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iturea” title=”Iturea”>Iturea</a>, <a href=”https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perea_(Bible)” title=”Perea (Bible)”>Perea</a>, and <a href=”https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Idumea” title=”Idumea”>Idumea</a>, and took the title &ldquo;<a href=”https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basileus” title=”Basileus”>basileus</a>&rdquo;. Some modern scholars refer to this period as an independent kingdom of <a href=”https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Land_of_Israel” title=”Land of Israel”>Israel</a>.<a href=”https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hasmonean_dynasty#cite_note-8”>[5]</a></p>

<hr /> <h3 id=”pharisee-and-sadducee-factions-…-this-his-here-thisis-now.”>Pharisee and Sadducee factions &hellip; this his here; thisis now.</h3>

<p>Kingdom at its greatest extent under <a href=”https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salome_Alexandra” title=”Salome Alexandra”>Salome Alexandra</a></p>

<p>It is difficult to state at what time the <a href=”https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pharisees” title=”Pharisees”>Pharisees</a>, as a party, arose. Josephus first mentions them in connection with Jonathan, the successor of Judas Maccabeus (&ldquo;Ant.&rdquo; xiii. 5, &sect; 9). One of the factors that distinguished the Pharisees from other groups prior to the destruction of the Temple was their belief that all Jews had to observe the purity laws (which applied to the Temple service) outside the Temple. The major difference, however, was the continued adherence of the Pharisees to the laws and traditions of the Jewish people in the face of assimilation. As Josephus noted, the Pharisees were considered the most expert and accurate expositors of Jewish law.</p>

<p>During the Hasmonean period, the <a href=”https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sadducees” title=”Sadducees”>Sadducees</a> and Pharisees functioned primarily as political parties. Although the Pharisees had opposed the wars of expansion of the Hasmoneans and the forced conversions of the Idumeans, the political rift between them became wider when Pharisees demanded that the Hasmonean king <a href=”https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexander_Jannaeus” title=”Alexander Jannaeus”>Alexander Jannaeus</a> choose between being king and being High Priest. In response, the king openly sided with the Sadducees by adopting their rites in the Temple. His actions caused a riot in the Temple and led to a brief civil war that ended with a bloody repression of the Pharisees, although at his deathbed the king called for a reconciliation between the two parties. Alexander was succeeded by his widow, <a href=”https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salome_Alexandra” title=”Salome Alexandra”>Salome Alexandra</a>, whose brother was <a href=”https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shimon_ben_Shetach” title=”Shimon ben Shetach”>Shimon ben Shetach</a>, a leading Pharisee. Upon her death her elder son, Hyrcanus, sought Pharisee support, and her younger son, Aristobulus, sought the support of the Sadducees. The conflict between Hyrcanus and Aristobulus culminated in a civil war that ended when the Roman general Pompey captured Jerusalem in 63 BCE and inaugurated the Roman period of Jewish history.</p>

<p>Josephus attests that Salome Alexandra was very favourably inclined toward the Pharisees and that their political influence grew tremendously under her reign, especially in the institution known as the <a href=”https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sanhedrin” title=”Sanhedrin”>Sanhedrin</a>. Later texts such as the Mishnah and the Talmud record a host of rulings ascribed to the Pharisees concerning sacrifices and other ritual practices in the Temple, torts, criminal law, and governance. The influence of the Pharisees over the lives of the common people remained strong, and their rulings on Jewish law were deemed authoritative by many. Although these texts were written long after these periods, many scholars believe that they are a fairly reliable account of history during the Second Temple era.</p>
